What to Expect at Arraignment in Tustin — Step-by-Step Guide

TL;DR

  • Arraignment under CA Penal Code §988 = procedural hearing, 5-15 minutes

  • Standard plea: NOT GUILTY — preserves all defense options
  • Tustin arraignments at Harbor Justice Center (misdemeanors) or Central Justice Center (felonies)
  • Judge addresses bail + may issue Criminal Protective Order (PC §136.2)
  • Post bail BEFORE arraignment — better outcomes than appearing from custody

The first court appearance after a Tustin arrest — the arraignment — is far simpler than most families fear. It is not a trial. It is not a sentencing hearing. It is a procedural step the court takes within 48 hours of every arrest. This guide explains exactly what happens, what to expect, and why posting bail BEFORE arraignment makes everything better.

What an Arraignment Actually Is

Under California Penal Code §988, the arraignment must include four elements:

  1. Reading the complaint or information aloud to the defendant
  2. Advising the defendant of the right to an attorney (and appointing a public defender if they cannot afford one)
  3. Advising of the right to a speedy trial
  4. Accepting a plea

The whole hearing typically lasts 5 to 15 minutes. For Tustin defendants, the arraignment is also when the prosecutor will likely request a Criminal Protective Order (for victim-known cases) and when the judge will set or confirm bail.

The 48-Hour Timeline (PC §825)

Under California Penal Code §825, the defendant must be arraigned within 48 hours of arrest, excluding Sundays and judicial holidays. This is rooted in Fourth Amendment protections against extended detention without judicial review.

  • Monday morning arrest → typically arraigned by Wednesday morning
  • Friday afternoon arrest → may not be arraigned until Monday or Tuesday (Sat/Sun don't count)
  • Holiday weekends extend the timeline further

The 48-hour clock does NOT prevent bail from being posted. As soon as booking is complete and the bail amount is on the schedule, a licensed bondsman can post the bond and the defendant is released — sometimes hours after arrest, days before the arraignment.

Step-by-Step: What Happens at the Hearing

  1. Defendant brought to courtroom. In custody clothes if not bailed out, regular clothes if released on bond.
  2. Judge confirms identity and reads the charge — for example, "Penal Code 273.5(a) corporal injury to a spouse" or "Vehicle Code 23152 driving under the influence."
  3. Defendant advised of constitutional rights: right to counsel, right to a jury trial, right against self-incrimination.
  4. Public defender appointed if the defendant cannot afford private counsel and meets indigency requirements.
  5. Plea entered. Almost always "not guilty" at arraignment so defense has time to review evidence.
  6. Bail addressed. The judge confirms or modifies the bail amount, may impose conditions (stay-away orders, drug testing, ankle monitor for higher-risk felonies).
  7. Criminal Protective Order (PC §136.2) issued if requested by the prosecutor — restricts contact with alleged victims for the duration of the case.
  8. Future court date set — typically a pretrial conference 30-45 days out.

Why "Not Guilty" Is Almost Always the Right Plea

Pleading guilty at arraignment surrenders all defense options before the defense attorney has reviewed:

  • Police reports and arrest documentation
  • Witness statements
  • Body-cam and dashcam footage
  • Potential constitutional violations (illegal search, Miranda issues, probable cause defects)
  • Diversion program eligibility (PC §§1000, 1001.36, 1001.80, 1001.95)
  • Charge reduction opportunities (PC §17(b) for wobblers)

The standard arraignment plea is "not guilty" — this preserves all options. Pleading guilty makes sense only after consulting a defense attorney who has reviewed the actual case.

Why Posting Bail BEFORE Arraignment Matters

  • Defendant arrives in regular clothes — not jail jumpsuit. Judges respond better to defendants who appear self-reliant and engaged.
  • Defense attorney can consult outside custody — uninterrupted, no jail visiting hours, better strategic preparation.
  • Family can prepare housing, work coverage, counseling before the hearing
  • CPO compliance is easier from outside custody — accidental violations during stressful release process are common when bail is posted at the last minute
  • Total custody time drops from days to hours when bail is posted before arraignment

What to Wear and Bring to Arraignment

  • Conservative business-appropriate clothing — no jeans, no sneakers, no logos
  • Clean appearance — judges form impressions in 5 minutes
  • All paperwork from the bondsman + any defense attorney correspondence
  • List of prior convictions if any (your defense attorney needs this for negotiations)
  • Proof of employment if relevant for OR release argument
  • No phones in courtroom (most California courthouses prohibit them in operation)

Frequently Asked Questions

What is an arraignment?
PC §988 — first court appearance after arrest. 5-15 min procedural hearing where court reads the charge, advises rights, accepts plea. Not a trial.
How soon after a Tustin arrest?
Within 48 hours per PC §825, excluding Sundays + judicial holidays. Friday arrests may not arraign until Monday/Tuesday.
What happens at the hearing?
Identity confirmed → charge read → rights advised → plea entered → bail addressed → CPO issued (if applicable) → future court date set.
Should I plead guilty at arraignment?
Almost never. Pleading guilty surrenders defense options before attorney has reviewed evidence, diversion eligibility, or constitutional defenses. Standard plea is NOT GUILTY.
Can I post bail before arraignment?
Yes — and you almost always should. Better outcomes than appearing from custody.
